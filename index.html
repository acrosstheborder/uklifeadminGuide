Section 1

Finding Accommodation in the UK

Most students begin in university-managed halls — a brilliant first choice. But for Year 2 onwards, summer stays, or private living, you'll need to navigate the wider housing market. Here's everything you need to know.

🏫
Start with University-Managed Halls

Why Halls Are the Best First Move

University-managed halls of residence are almost always the smartest first-year choice. Bills are often included (electricity, internet, water), maintenance is handled for you, and you're surrounded by other students navigating the same experience. Most universities guarantee a place for first-year international students — check your university's accommodation portal as soon as you accept your offer.

Apply to university accommodation as early as possible — places fill up fast, especially for international students arriving late.
→ UCAS Student Accommodation Guide

Purpose-Built Student Accommodation (PBSA)

When university halls aren't available or you want something more independent, Purpose-Built Student Accommodation is the next best thing. These are professionally managed buildings designed specifically for students — all bills included, communal spaces, and 24-hour security.

🏢

The UK's largest PBSA provider with properties across 25+ cities. All-inclusive bills, high-speed WiFi, study rooms, and summer stay options. Ideal for students who want certainty and simplicity.

🔑
Private Rentals — What to Know

Navigating the Private Rental Market

From Year 2 onwards, many students move into private housing — typically shared houses known as HMOs (Houses in Multiple Occupation). The key platforms are Rightmove and Zoopla. Use the "Student" or "HMO" filter when searching, and always view properties in person before signing anything.

For single rooms or short-term summer lets, SpareRoom is the most popular platform. Use "short term" or "summer" tags to filter appropriately.

⚠️

Right to Rent — This is a Legal Requirement

In England, landlords must verify your immigration status before you move in. You will need to provide a Share Code (generated from your eVisa or BRP) to prove your right to rent. Generate yours at gov.uk/view-right-to-rent ↗

📋
What to check before signing a tenancy agreement
▾

Before you sign anything, read the tenancy agreement carefully. Key things to check: how long is the contract (usually 12 months), is there a break clause, who is responsible for repairs, what is included in the rent, and what is the deposit amount.

Deposit protection: Your landlord is legally required to protect your deposit in a government-approved scheme within 30 days. The three schemes are DPS, MyDeposits, and TDS. If they don't, you can claim compensation.

Useful toolShelter England has a free tenancy agreement checker: shelter.org.uk ↗
🏖️
Finding summer accommodation between academic years
▾

University halls typically end contracts in June or July. If you need somewhere to stay over summer — especially if you're working or doing a placement — your options are: extending your PBSA contract (Unite and iQ both offer this), finding a summer sub-let on SpareRoom, or checking if your university offers a reduced-rate summer stay programme.

Pro tipSome universities offer international students priority access to summer accommodation. Email your accommodation office in February or March — don't leave it until May.
💡
Bills, utilities, and what "all-inclusive" actually means
▾

PBSA and university halls typically include electricity, heating, water, and WiFi in the rent. In private rentals, you usually pay rent separately from bills. A common arrangement is to use a bill-splitting service like Splittable or Splitwise with housemates.

Energy bills: Compare tariffs at Uswitch or MoneySuperMarket. For broadband, Save the Student regularly updates the cheapest student broadband deals.

RememberEven in private rentals, full-time students are exempt from Council Tax. You'll still need to apply — see the Life Admin section below.

Work Rights & Employment

As an international student, your BRP or digital immigration status (eVisa) defines exactly how many hours you can work. Getting this right is critical — breaches can have serious visa consequences.

Your Working Hours at a Glance

Study LevelTerm-Time LimitVacation / BreaksWeek Runs
Degree Level (undergraduate & postgraduate)20 hours per weekFull-time (no limit)Monday to Sunday
Below Degree Level (foundation, A-level equivalent)10 hours per weekFull-time (no limit)Monday to Sunday
All students — vacation periodsNo restrictionFull-time permittedMonday to Sunday

⚠️ The Home Office counts your working week Monday to Sunday — not Monday to Friday. Exceeding your limit by even one hour is technically a visa breach. Keep a weekly record of your hours.

🖥️

Proving Your Right to Work

Every employer in the UK is legally required to check that you have the right to work before you start. You generate a Share Code which your employer uses to verify your status online — you don't hand over your physical BRP.

Generate your Share Code at:

→ gov.uk/prove-right-to-work
Share Codes expire after 90 days. Generate a fresh one when starting each new job.
🔢

National Insurance (NI) Number

A National Insurance number is your unique identifier for the UK tax and benefits system. You must have one to work legally and ensure your tax contributions are recorded correctly against your name.

Apply online — it's free and takes around 20 minutes:

→ Apply for NI Number
Apply as soon as you arrive — processing can take several weeks and you'll need it before your first payslip.
💰
Understanding your payslip and tax codes
▾

Your payslip will show your gross pay (before tax) and net pay (after tax and National Insurance deductions). Your tax code tells your employer how much income tax to deduct. The most common code for students is 1257L, which means you can earn up to £12,570 per year before paying income tax.

If you think you've been taxed incorrectly, contact HMRC directly or use the GOV.UK income tax calculator to check.

Useful linkCheck your current income tax — gov.uk ↗
📍
Where to find part-time jobs as an international student
▾

The best starting points for part-time work: your university's own jobs board (usually listed in the student portal), Indeed.co.uk, LinkedIn Jobs, and Totaljobs. For casual or flexible work, apps like TaskRabbit, Tempr, and StudentJob.co.uk are worth checking.

On-campus jobs are easiest firstUniversity libraries, cafeterias, student unions, and admin departments all hire students. They're flexible, understand your schedule, and guaranteed to be term-time aware.
🎓
Placements, internships and graduate schemes
▾

A placement year (usually Year 3 of a 4-year course) or a summer internship can dramatically improve your graduate prospects. Most major UK employers run formal schemes — apply early, usually in October or November of the year before the placement.

Key platforms: RateMyPlacement, Prospects.ac.uk, Milkround, and your university careers service. Many graduate schemes also accept applications from international students — always check the visa sponsorship status before applying.

Visa sponsorshipAfter graduation, the Graduate Visa allows you to stay in the UK for 2 years (3 years for PhD graduates) to work or look for work. Your employer may then sponsor a Skilled Worker visa. Learn more ↗

"Keeping a simple weekly spreadsheet of your work hours is the single most important thing you can do to protect your visa. It takes 2 minutes and could save you everything."

Section 3

The UK Academic Calendar

The UK academic year follows a three-term structure. Understanding this is essential because your working rights change during vacations — and knowing when exams and deadlines cluster helps you plan your life far in advance.

🍂

Autumn Term (Semester 1)

Roughly: Late September to mid-December

This is when your academic year begins. Freshers' Week (or Welcome Week) is the very first week — a series of social events, orientations, and enrolment activities. Your first set of assessments or coursework deadlines usually fall in November or December.

Christmas vacation (mid-December to mid-January) counts as a full vacation — you can work full-time during this period.
🌱

Spring Term (Semester 2)

Roughly: Mid-January to late March

The second term usually ends before Easter. Some universities have a "Reading Week" mid-February — this is still technically term-time, so your 20-hour work limit still applies. Do not work full-time during Reading Week.

Easter vacation (late March to late April) is a full vacation period — full-time work permitted.
☀️

Summer Term

Roughly: Late April to mid-June

The summer term is primarily for exams and dissertation submissions. Teaching has usually ended, but if you are still registered as a student and your institution is in term-time, your 20-hour limit still applies until your last official exam date.

Summer vacation (from your final exam onwards) is your longest vacation — full-time work permitted until late September.
📌

Always Check Your University's Specific Dates

Every university sets its own term dates and these can vary by several weeks. Always find the "Key Dates" or "Term Dates" page on your university's website and save it somewhere you can easily access. Never assume you're in vacation based on a general estimate — verify it.

🏛️
Council Tax Exemption

Full-Time Students Don't Pay Council Tax

Council Tax is a local authority charge that funds services like rubbish collection, libraries, and emergency services. Full-time students are legally exempt — but the exemption is not automatic. You need to actively apply.

How to apply: Download a "Student Status Letter" or "Council Tax Exemption Certificate" from your university's student portal (usually under "Student Services" or "My Documents"). Then submit it to your local council. Search for your council at gov.uk ↗.

⚠️

Mixed Households

If you live with even one non-student (e.g. a working professional or gap-year housemate), the household loses its full exemption. The non-student will still have to pay — but your exemption as a student still reduces their bill by 25%.

📺
TV Licensing — What You Need to Know

Do You Actually Need a TV Licence?

This confuses a lot of students. The TV Licence (currently £174.50 per year) is legally required if you watch any live television on any device — a phone, laptop, tablet, or TV — regardless of which channel. It also applies if you use BBC iPlayer, even to watch programmes after they've aired.

You do NOT need a licence if you only use Netflix, Disney+, Amazon Prime, YouTube, or any other streaming service that doesn't include live TV. However, you must declare this on the TV Licensing website to stop receiving enforcement letters.

Section 5

Money, Transfers & Budgeting

Managing money across two countries, two currencies, and two banking systems is one of the real practical challenges of being an international student. Here's how to make your money go further.

💸
Sending & Receiving Money from Home

Never Use a Bank Transfer to Send Money Internationally

Traditional bank-to-bank international transfers are one of the most expensive ways to move money. Banks typically add a margin of 3–5% on top of the mid-market exchange rate — which on a transfer of £5,000 could cost you £150–£250 in hidden fees alone.

Instead, use a specialist money transfer service:

💚 Wise (formerly TransferWise)

Uses the real mid-market rate with a small, transparent fee. One of the cheapest options available. Also offers a multi-currency account and debit card.

wise.com ↗
💙 Atlantic Money

Flat fee of £3 per transfer regardless of amount — makes it especially cost-effective for larger transfers from home. Available on iOS and Android.

atlanticmoney.com ↗
🤝

Splitting Bills with Housemates

Splitwise is the standard UK tool for tracking shared house expenses — cleaning supplies, toilet roll, milk, internet, and other shared costs. Everyone adds what they spend and the app calculates who owes whom at the end of the month. Free, simple, and saves arguments.

📊

Student Budgeting Tools

Monzo and Revolut both have built-in budgeting features that automatically categorise your spending — groceries, eating out, transport, entertainment. Setting monthly spending limits takes 2 minutes and prevents the common end-of-term money panic.

🛒

Food Shopping on a Budget

For affordable grocery shopping, Aldi and Lidl are consistently the cheapest. Tesco Clubcard and Sainsbury's Nectar loyalty schemes offer regular discounts. The Too Good To Go app lets you buy surplus food from restaurants and cafés at 70–90% off.

💳

The Best Student Bank Accounts in the UK

Traditional high-street banks (Barclays, HSBC, NatWest, Santander) offer student accounts with interest-free overdrafts of up to £1,500 — useful in emergencies. For quick setup without a UK credit history, start with Monzo or Revolut, then apply for a student account once you have your university enrollment letter. Compare accounts at Save the Student ↗

Useful Tech & Student Discounts

Being a student in the UK unlocks hundreds of pounds of savings per year. These are the tools and apps that every international student should have set up in their first week — most are completely free.

Free Discount Platforms — Set These Up Immediately

UNiDAYS
FREE

Verify your student email and get instant discount codes for Apple, Samsung, ASOS, Nike, Deliveroo, and dozens more. One of the most widely accepted student discount platforms in the UK.

myunidays.com ↗
Student Beans
FREE

Similar to UNiDAYS but with different brand partnerships. Worth having both — some brands are on Student Beans only. Also offers discount codes for software like Adobe, Spotify, and Microsoft 365.

studentbeans.com ↗
TOTUM Card
From £14.99/yr

Unlike the free apps, TOTUM is a physical card. Its key advantage: it includes a PASS hologram — a legally recognised proof-of-age document in the UK. Use it at pubs, clubs, and anywhere that requires age verification instead of carrying your passport.

totum.com ↗
16–25 Railcard
£30/yr

Saves you ⅓ off all rail travel in the UK. If you take the train more than a handful of times per year, this pays for itself very quickly. Also available as a 3-year card for £70.

16-25railcard.co.uk ↗
Spotify Student
~£5.99/mo

Spotify Premium at half price for students — includes Hulu in the US but in the UK includes Spotify Premium only. Verify via Student Beans or SheerID. Widely available at most universities.

spotify.com/uk/student ↗
Apple / Microsoft Student
Up to 25% off

Apple offers student pricing on Macs, iPads, and accessories via UNiDAYS. Microsoft 365 is often free through your university — check your student portal. Adobe Creative Cloud also offers a significant student discount.

Apple Education Store ↗

Practical Life Apps Every Student Needs

👗

Second-Hand Shopping: Vinted & Depop

Vinted and Depop are the go-to platforms for buying affordable second-hand clothing, winter coats, kitchen equipment, and household items. Arriving in autumn and need a warm coat? You can find quality second-hand options for £10–£30 rather than spending £80+ in a shop.

📱

NHS App

Once registered with a GP, download the NHS App to book and manage appointments, access prescriptions, view your health record, and get COVID certificates. It's also the quickest way to request a GP appointment without calling the surgery directly.

🧺

Splitwise — Shared House Finance

Splitwise tracks shared expenses with housemates automatically — who bought the washing up liquid, who paid the electricity top-up, who owes who for the takeaway. Prevents misunderstandings and keeps finances transparent between everyone in the house.

Major food delivery platforms now offer significant student deals — some of the best value subscriptions available. Here's exactly what each platform offers and how to access the discount.

🛵 Deliveroo Students
FREE FOR 3 YEARS

Verified students get Deliveroo Plus Silver free for up to three years — one of the best student deals available in the UK. Includes free delivery on restaurant orders over £15 and grocery orders over £25. Also includes exclusive 10% discounts at popular chains including Wingstop.

Verify through UNiDAYS or Student Beans. Available to students at UK universities.

Up to 50% off membership

Uber Eats frequently offers 50% off Uber One memberships via UNiDAYS — bringing the monthly cost significantly down. Uber One includes £0 delivery fees and up to 10% off "Uber One" marked orders.

New customer codes: Often £10 off your first two orders. Always check the app or UNiDAYS for the latest codes before your first order.

ubereats.com ↗
🍕 Just Eat
~£5.99 / 90 days

Check for student-specific codes on Student Beans before purchasing — discounted rates are sometimes available.

just-eat.co.uk ↗
🍽️

Cooking at Home Will Save You Significantly More

Even with student discounts, regular food delivery adds up fast. A weekly meal plan — even a simple one — can cut your food spend by 60–70% compared to ordering regularly. The BBC Good Food website has a large collection of cheap, easy student meals. Batch cooking on Sundays (pasta sauces, rice dishes, soups) is the single most effective money-saving habit you can build.

"The gap between what students spend and what they could spend, without any reduction in quality of life, is almost always food and subscriptions. A weekly meal plan and one shared streaming account is worth more than any part-time job shift."

— Across the Border CIC
